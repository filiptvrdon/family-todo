# ADHD/Neurodivergent-Friendly Design Principles

One of the users may have ADHD or a similar condition, so this is a first-class design requirement, not an afterthought. Every feature and UI decision should be evaluated through this lens.

---

## Reduce friction and overwhelm

- Single "what do I do next?" view — one task surfaced, not a list to scroll through
- Auto-prioritization so users don't have to decide what matters right now
- Keep the default view minimal — don't show everything at once
- Avoid long lists; prefer chunked, focused views (e.g. "today", "just 3 things")
- No guilt-inducing UI — overdue tasks should not feel punishing

## Quick capture (brain dump mode)

- Add a task in 2 seconds — no categorization required at entry time
- **Natural language input:** "Call dentist tomorrow at 3pm", "Pay rent every 1st, remind me the day before"
- **AI-assisted entry (preferred):** One prompt — *"What's on your mind?"* — AI extracts task, date, priority, recurrence quietly in the background; user sees a simple confirmation card, not a form
- A separate "review inbox" step handles organizing what was captured — never block capture on organization
- Templates for recurring task types reduce repeated decisions

## Task creation UX

The goal is **zero friction at the point of capture, full power available when wanted**.

Approach: **natural language + progressive disclosure**
1. One text box: *"What's on your mind?"*
2. AI parses intent and fills in details quietly
3. User sees a simple confirmation card — not a form
4. Advanced options (scheduling, recurrence, energy level) are accessible but never mandatory

Never require categorization before saving. Never make the form feel like work.

## Make completion feel rewarding

- Celebrate task completions — animations, positive micro-copy, streaks
- Acknowledge effort, not just output (partial progress counts)
- Make even tiny tasks feel satisfying to check off
- Use warm, encouraging language throughout — never clinical or neutral
- Avoid shame spirals when tasks are missed or overdue

## Support task initiation (the hardest part for ADHD)

- Surface a clear "what should I do next?" answer — one task, not a list
- Allow tasks to be broken into very small, completable steps
- Support focus sessions: Pomodoro-style timers or "body doubling" reminders
- Gentle nudges/reminders, never nagging
- "Commitment devices" — optional stakes or accountability prompts

## Time & planning

- Time estimates with chunking — breaking tasks into small, completable pieces
- Deadlines that work backwards: "to finish by Friday, start Wednesday"
- Time blocking: optionally scheduling tasks into actual calendar slots
- Avoid requiring time estimates — they're optional enrichment, not mandatory fields

## Reduce cognitive load

- Minimal required fields when creating a task
- Smart defaults everywhere
- Clear visual hierarchy; avoid clutter
- Energy-level tagging: some tasks need high focus, others are low-effort — surface appropriate tasks based on context

## Forgiveness and flexibility

- "I can't deal with this right now" — guilt-free snooze without friction
- Easy to reschedule without shame
- No permanent "failed" states — tasks roll over gracefully
- Archive instead of delete where possible
- Missed tasks are "waiting for you when you're ready", never "overdue failures"

## Keep engagement alive

- Dopamine-friendly progress feedback: streaks, completion animations, celebratory moments
- Progress visibility for both partners — seeing what your partner completed is motivating
- Positive reinforcement for teamwork (e.g. "You two crushed it this week!")
- Gamification elements are welcome (points, streaks, shared achievements) — keep them opt-in and never punishing

## External accountability (partner layer)

- Sharing progress with each other is a built-in feature, not an afterthought
- Partner attribution: light and celebratory — "Filip just finished the dishes!"
- Joint wins are surfaced and celebrated — the team succeeding together is the core loop
